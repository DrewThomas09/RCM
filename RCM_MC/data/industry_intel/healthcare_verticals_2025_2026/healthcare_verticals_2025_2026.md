## TL;DR

- Across 17 specialized verticals, the dominant theme for 2025–2026 is regulatory recalibration under the second Trump administration: the federal SNF staffing mandate was vacated/rescinded, home health absorbed its first PDGM behavioral cut (−1.3% net for CY2026), hospital-at-home won a five-year extension to Sept 30, 2030, and the dialysis ETC model was terminated Dec 31, 2025 — while per-diem/bundled payment systems (PDPM, PDGM, hospice per-diem, ESRD PPS) continue to govern the economics.
- The data is highly chart-ready: each vertical has authoritative federal/industry datasets (CMS payment rules and cost reports, Care Compare, NIC MAP, USRDS, SAMHSA, MedPAC, CDC/NCHS ART, PHI) supporting specific per-unit economics — SNF ~$700–800/patient-day, hospice routine home care ~$224/day, ESRD $281.71/treatment, IVF $15,000–$20,000/cycle, NEMT $18–65/trip, gene therapies $373,000–$4.25M/dose.
- The verticals split into mature consolidated duopolies/oligopolies (dialysis: DaVita+Fresenius ~77% of facilities; NEMT brokers: ModivCare+MTM) and fragmented, demographically-driven growth markets (senior living, home health, ABA, behavioral health) where labor shortages and Medicaid dependence are the binding constraints.

## Key Findings

1. **Payment-rate environment for FY/CY2026 is modestly positive but below cost inflation in most post-acute settings.** SNF +3.2% (FY2026), hospice +2.6%, ESRD +2.2%, home health −1.3% net (the first PDGM cut to bite).
2. **The SNF federal minimum staffing mandate is dead.** A Texas court vacated key provisions (April 2025), the 2025 reconciliation law delayed it to 2034, and the Trump administration rescinded it (December 2025).
3. **Hospital-at-home secured long-term certainty** via the Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2026, extending the waiver to September 30, 2030 after a disruptive lapse during the 43-day 2025 government shutdown.
4. **Consolidation is extreme in dialysis and NEMT** but the SNF, hospice, home health, ABA, and fertility verticals remain fragmented with active roll-up activity.
5. **Gene/cell therapy pricing has reached $4.25M** (Lenmeldy), but 2025 marked the first major commercial failures: Pfizer confirmed on Feb 20, 2025 it would "cease further development and commercialization" of Beqvez, and BioMarin announced Oct 27, 2025 it would divest Roctavian (2025 sales $36M, up from $26M in 2024), recording ~$240M in Q4 2025 charges ($119M inventory write-off + $118M asset impairments).
6. **Labor is the universal binding constraint** — 5.4 million direct care workers at a $17.36 median hourly wage (2024), behavioral health workforce shortages (HRSA projects a national shortage of 43,660 adult psychiatrists by 2038), and SNF/home health staffing.

## GROUP A — LONG-TERM CARE & AGING SERVICES

### 1. Skilled Nursing Facilities (SNFs) / Nursing Homes

**(a) Structure & mechanics.** SNFs are paid under the SNF Prospective Payment System (PPS) using the Patient-Driven Payment Model (PDPM), implemented FY2020. PDPM divides the unadjusted federal per-diem into six components: five case-mix-adjusted (Physical Therapy, Occupational Therapy, Speech-Language Pathology, Nursing, Non-Therapy Ancillaries) plus one non-case-mix component. Primary diagnosis (ICD-10-CM) drives clinical category assignment. Medicare Part A covers up to 100 days post-qualifying-hospital-stay; Medicaid covers long-term custodial care. The SNF Value-Based Purchasing (VBP) program withholds 2% of Medicare FFS Part A payments and redistributes 50–70% based on performance.

**(b) Market size & volume.** ~15,000 Medicare/Medicaid-certified nursing facilities; ~1.2 million residents (July 2024). Total resident count declined ~10% over the past decade. Median facility had 100 beds in 2024 (10th percentile ≤50 beds; 90th percentile ≥176 beds).

**(c) Company counts & market share.** Highly fragmented. 73% for-profit, 20% non-profit, 7% government-owned. The 25 largest chains operate ~19% of facilities; single operators ~40%. Major operators include Ensign Group, Genesis Healthcare, and others; major REITs include Omega Healthcare Investors, Welltower, Ventas, Sabra, CareTrust. Since 2020, 774 nursing homes closed, losing 62,567 beds and displacing 28,421 residents.

**(d) Financial & operational benchmarks.** National median occupancy 80.2% (2023), up from 76.3% (2022); NIC MAP nursing care occupancy 84.5% (Q3 2024, >467,000 occupied beds across 31 primary markets). Payer mix: Medicaid primary for 63% of residents, Medicare 14%, other 23%. Residents receive an average of 3.85 hours of nursing care/day (0.68 RN, 0.87 LPN, 2.3 nurse aide) — down 7% from 4.13 hours in July 2015. Direct care nursing expense reached $49.2 billion (2023). Risk-adjusted community discharge rate 51.3% (2023–24); potentially preventable readmissions 10.7%. Five-Star Quality Rating system on Care Compare.

**(e) Reimbursement/policy 2025–2026.** FY2026 SNF PPS final rule (CMS-1827-F, effective Oct 1, 2025): +3.2% net update (3.3% market basket +0.6% forecast error −0.7% productivity), ~$1.16 billion increase. VBP reductions ~$208.36 million. 34 PDPM ICD-10 mapping changes (33 codes moved to "Return to Provider," including Type 1 diabetes, hypoglycemia, obesity, anorexia, bulimia). The federal minimum staffing mandate (finalized April 2024; KFF estimated only 19% of facilities met standards) was vacated by a Texas court (April 2025), delayed to 2034 by the 2025 reconciliation law, and rescinded by the Trump administration (December 2025).

**(f) Key data sources.** CMS SNF PPS final rules; CMS Care Compare/Nursing Home Compare; CASPER; Medicare cost reports; MedPAC March reports; KFF; CLA SNF Cost Comparison report; NIC MAP.

**Chart suggestions:** SNF payer mix → stacked bar (Medicaid 63%/Medicare 14%/other 23%); occupancy recovery 2022–2025 → line; ownership split → pie; PDPM six-component breakdown → stacked bar; nursing hours/resident-day → bar.

### 2. Assisted Living & Senior Living

**(a) Structure & mechanics.** Predominantly private-pay. The continuum: active adult (55+), independent living, assisted living, memory care, and continuing care retirement communities (CCRCs). Some assisted living covered by Medicaid HCBS waivers; nursing care covered by Medicaid. Revenue is monthly rent plus care/service fees.

**(b) Market size & volume.** NIC MAP tracks 31 primary markets (recently expanded to 214 markets, ~85% of US supply, >15,000 properties). Occupied senior housing units (IL+AL incl. memory care) reached ~635,000 in Q4 2025 across the 31 primary markets (up nearly 20,000 in 2025, a >3% gain). NIC MAP projects need for 549,000 additional units by 2028 and 806,000 by 2030.

**(c) Company counts & market share.** Major operators include Brookdale Senior Living (largest), Atria Senior Living, Sunrise Senior Living, Holiday by Atria, LCS, Five Star. REITs: Welltower, Ventas, American Healthcare REIT. Fragmented below the top operators.

**(d) Financial & operational benchmarks.** Senior housing occupancy ended 2025 at 89.1% (18th consecutive quarterly increase), reaching 89.5% in Q1 2026. Independent living >90% (91.1% Q1 2026); assisted living 87.7% (Q4 2025)/87.9% (Q1 2026); active adult ~92%. Rent growth 4–5% YoY. Average operating margins surpassed 25% in mid-2025 (highest since 2018). Highest-occupancy markets Q1 2026: Boston 93.6%, Baltimore 91.8%, San Francisco 91.6%; lowest: Atlanta 86%, Miami 86.2%, Las Vegas 87%.

**(e) Reimbursement/policy.** Private-pay so largely insulated from CMS rate-setting; exposed to Medicaid HCBS for AL waiver beds. Supply constrained by construction costs and capital availability — inventory growth fell to 0.7% YoY (lowest since NIC MAP tracking began in 2006). The first Baby Boomers turned 80 in 2026.

**(f) Key data sources.** NIC MAP / NIC MAP Vision; McKnight's Senior Living; company filings (Brookdale, Welltower, Ventas).

**Chart suggestions:** Senior living occupancy by property type 2019–2026 → line; occupancy by market → bar/map; supply gap projection (156K→549K→806K) → bar; rent growth → line.

### 3. Hospice

**(a) Structure & mechanics.** Medicare hospice benefit (Part A) pays a per-diem across four levels of care: Routine Home Care (RHC), Continuous Home Care (CHC), Inpatient Respite Care (IRC), and General Inpatient Care (GIP). RHC uses a two-tier rate (days 1–60 higher, days 61+ lower) plus a Service Intensity Add-on (SIA) paying RN/social-worker visits in the last 7 days of life (up to 4 hours/day). Subject to an annual aggregate cap per beneficiary. Eligibility requires terminal prognosis ≤6 months.

**(b) Market size & volume.** ~1.72 million Medicare beneficiaries received hospice in 2023; 51.7% of Medicare decedents used the benefit. Medicare hospice spending ~$26 billion (2023). Alzheimer's/dementia is the largest diagnosis category (~24–25%).

**(c) Company counts & market share.** ~73% for-profit by 2024 (up from ~5% in 1992). The ten largest chains treat roughly a third of patients. Major players: VITAS (Chemed), Optum/Amedisys, Gentiva (former Kindred at Home hospice), AccentCare, Compassus, Agape. PE-driven consolidation.

**(d) Financial & operational benchmarks.** Median patient enters with ~17 days left; lifetime length of stay averages 187 days for-profit vs. 130 days non-profit. MedPAC reported aggregate Medicare hospice margins ~16% (2022). Median live-discharge rate ~18% (2018); 10% of providers exceed 42%. Neurological diagnoses have longest LOS (159 days); COPD 135 days.

**(e) Reimbursement/policy 2025–2026.** FY2026 final rule (CMS-1835-F): +2.6% payment update (~$750 million), aggregate cap $35,361.44 (up from $34,465.34). HOPE tool replaced HIS Oct 1, 2025. Non-reporting penalty −4 points (net −1.4%). Hospice Special Focus Program launched 2024 (monitors worst-performing ~1%).

**(f) Key data sources.** CMS hospice final rules; NHPCO Facts & Figures; MedPAC March reports (Ch. 9); CMS hospice claims; Hospice Care Index on Care Compare.

**Chart suggestions:** Hospice LOS distribution → histogram; for-profit share over time (5%→73%) → line; four levels of care per-diem rates → bar; live-discharge rate distribution → box plot; diagnosis mix → pie.

### 4. Home Health

**(a) Structure & mechanics.** Home Health PPS uses the Patient-Driven Groupings Model (PDGM, since Jan 1, 2020) with a 30-day unit of payment and 432 payment groups, each with case-mix weight and Low Utilization Payment Adjustment (LUPA) threshold. Below the LUPA visit threshold, payment reverts to per-visit rates (difference often $500–$1,500/period). Requires a face-to-face encounter; NPs, CNSs, PAs may now order/certify (CARES Act).

**(b) Market size & volume.** ~11,000+ agencies. Since PDGM, home health utilization dropped by over 2 million beneficiaries (2018–2024). Los Angeles County alone added 1,300+ new agencies since 2020 (9.4% of national Medicare home health spending), driving fraud concerns.

**(c) Company counts & market share.** Major players: UnitedHealth/Optum (LHC Group + Amedisys), Enhabit, Aveanna, BAYADA. Fragmented with active consolidation.

**(d) Financial & operational benchmarks.** The 30-day period payment is the core economic unit. CMS runs a permanent/temporary behavioral adjustment to recoup ~$5.3 billion of overpayments (cumulative through CY24).

**(e) Reimbursement/policy 2025–2026.** CY2026 HH PPS final rule (CMS-1828-F, finalized Nov 28, 2025): −1.3% net (−$220 million) vs. the proposed −6.4% (−$1.135 billion). Composed of +2.4% market basket, −1.023% permanent PDGM adjustment, −3.0% temporary adjustment, −0.1% FDL. CMS recalculated the permanent adjustment using only CY2020–2022 data (after stakeholders argued post-2022 behavior change reflected OASIS-E, HHVBP, and MA penetration, not PDGM). OASIS required for all-payer skilled patients since July 1, 2025; OASIS-E2 effective April 1, 2026. HHVBP scorecard reweighted (40% OASIS outcomes/40% claims/20% HHCAHPS).

**(f) Key data sources.** CMS HH PPS final rules; Home Health Compare; MedPAC; CMS home health claims; PDGM grouper files.

**Chart suggestions:** 30-day period economics (full vs. LUPA) → bar; CY2026 rate build-up (waterfall: +2.4%/−1.023%/−3.0%/−0.1%); utilization decline 2018–2024 → line; PDGM 432-group structure → treemap.

## GROUP B — BEHAVIORAL HEALTH & SUBSTANCE USE

### 5. Behavioral / Mental Health Treatment

**(a) Structure & mechanics.** Mix of outpatient therapy (psychotherapy CPT 90832/90834/90837), psychiatry (E/M + add-on codes), intensive outpatient (IOP), partial hospitalization (PHP), and inpatient psychiatric. Reimbursed via commercial, Medicaid, Medicare. Mental Health Parity and Addiction Equity Act (MHPAEA) parity enforcement effective January 2025. Telehealth plays an outsized role post-COVID.

**(b) Market size & volume.** Per SAMHSA 2023 NSDUH, 48.5 million Americans had a substance use disorder and a large mental health treatment population. Demand for behavioral health services projected to increase 49% through 2033 while supply grows only 11% (HRSA).

**(c) Company counts & market share.** Fragmented; mix of solo/group practices, hospital systems, Certified Community Behavioral Health Clinics (CCBHCs), and platforms (e.g., LifeStance, SonderMind, Headway, Talkiatry, Brightside).

**(d) Financial & operational benchmarks.** Workforce shortage is the binding constraint. As of December 2025, ~40% of US population lives in mental health HPSAs. Per HRSA's Bureau of Health Workforce, the projected national shortage of adult psychiatrists by 2038 is 43,660 (Jan 15, 2026 NCHWA update), and an additional 136,350 psychologists would be needed by 2038 to meet all unmet need (HRSA "State of the Behavioral Health Workforce, 2025"). Under elevated-need scenarios, child/adolescent psychiatrist shortage reaches 19,770 (36% adequacy) and marriage & family therapist deficit 63,540 (45% adequacy).

**(e) Reimbursement/policy 2025–2026.** MHPAEA parity enforcement (Jan 2025) removing prior-auth hurdles; Medicare now covers marriage/family therapists and mental health counselors (2024); telehealth flexibilities extended.

**(f) Key data sources.** SAMHSA NSDUH; HRSA Bureau of Health Workforce / NCHWA; KFF; HRSA HPSA data.

**Chart suggestions:** Behavioral health workforce shortage by discipline → bar; HPSA coverage → map; demand vs. supply growth (49% vs. 11%) → line; telehealth share of visits → line.

### 6. Substance Use Disorder (SUD) / Addiction Treatment

**(a) Structure & mechanics.** Care organized along the ASAM continuum: Level 0.5 (early intervention), Level 1 (outpatient), Level 2 (intensive outpatient/partial hospitalization), Level 3 (residential/inpatient), Level 4 (medically managed intensive inpatient). Opioid Treatment Programs (OTPs) dispense methadone under 42 CFR Part 8 (revised October 2024 to eliminate the one-year history requirement and allow telehealth MAT initiation). MAT uses methadone, buprenorphine, naltrexone.

**(b) Market size & volume.** ~1,900 operating OTPs (2022), up from ~1,519 in 2018. 311,531 clients received methadone in OTP facilities (2020, N-SSATS). In 2023, 48.5 million Americans had SUD but only 23.6% accessed treatment.

**(c) Company counts & market share.** Major MAT/OTP players: BayMark Health Services, Behavioral Health Group (BHG), Crossroads Treatment Centers, Acadia Healthcare. Residential: American Addiction Centers, Recovery Village, Landmark Recovery.

**(d) Financial & operational benchmarks.** Per-diem residential economics; MAT monthly drug costs — Suboxone (buprenorphine/naloxone) $400–600/mo branded ($200–350 generic); Vivitrol (naltrexone) $1,000+/monthly dose; Antabuse $30–100/mo; Campral $150–300/mo. Continuous MAT halves overdose mortality vs. abstinence-only care.

**(e) Reimbursement/policy 2025–2026.** Revised 42 CFR Part 8 (Oct 2024) permanently expanded methadone/buprenorphine telehealth access; buprenorphine X-waiver eliminated (expanding prescriber pool from specialists to primary care); parity enforcement (Jan 2025).

**(f) Key data sources.** SAMHSA N-SSATS/NSDUH; AATOD; CMS OTP payment data; ASAM criteria.

**Chart suggestions:** ASAM levels of care → tiered diagram; OTP count growth (1,519→1,900) → line; treatment gap (48.5M with SUD vs. 23.6% treated) → bar; MAT drug costs → bar.

### 7. Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) for Autism

**(a) Structure & mechanics.** ABA billed via Category I CPT codes 97151–97158 plus Category III 0362T/0373T, in 15-minute units. 97151 = behavior-identification assessment (BCBA/QHP, includes face-to-face + non-face-to-face report writing); 97152 = supporting assessment by technician; 97153 = adaptive behavior treatment by technician (RBT) under supervision; 97154 = group treatment by technician; 97155 = treatment with protocol modification by QHP; 97156 = family guidance; 97157 = multiple-family group guidance; 97158 = group treatment by QHP. Modifiers HN (bachelor's/RBT/BCaBA), HO (master's), HP (doctoral) affect reimbursement. Requires prior authorization. ICD-10 F84.0 (autistic disorder).

**(b) Market size & volume.** Driven by autism prevalence — 1 in 31 (3.2%) of 8-year-olds per CDC's ADDM Network 2022 data released April 15, 2025 (up from 1 in 36 in 2020) — and state insurance mandates (all 50 states mandate some autism/ABA coverage).

**(c) Company counts & market share.** Fragmented but consolidating; players include Centria Autism, Hopebridge, Action Behavior Centers, LEARN Behavioral, Autism Learning Partners. PE-heavy roll-up.

**(d) Financial & operational benchmarks.** Per-hour (per-unit) economics; workforce of BCBAs (Board Certified Behavior Analysts) and RBTs (Registered Behavior Technicians). RBT-delivered 97153 paired with BCBA supervision 97155 (cannot be billed concurrently by a single QHP). 97151 typically capped at 8 units (2 hours)/day; commercial claim filing windows 60–180 days.

**(e) Reimbursement/policy 2025–2026.** Medicaid (EPSDT mandate for children) and commercial mandates; state-level changes ongoing (e.g., Washington Medicaid ABA billing guide Feb 2025; New Hampshire Medicaid proposed 97155 changes July 2025).

**(f) Key data sources.** ABA Coding Coalition (abacodes.org); CASP Guidelines; AMA CPT; state Medicaid manuals; Behavior Analyst Certification Board (BACB) for workforce counts.

**Chart suggestions:** CPT code structure (97151–97158) → table; provider qualification/modifier tiers → bar; per-hour economics by code → bar.

## GROUP C — HOME-BASED & POST-ACUTE CARE

### 8. Hospital-at-Home / Home-Based Acute Care

**(a) Structure & mechanics.** CMS Acute Hospital Care at Home (AHCAH) initiative launched November 2020 under COVID-19 waiver authority, waiving the Medicare Condition of Participation (§482.23(b)) requiring 24/7 on-site nursing. Approved hospitals deliver inpatient-level care at home with DRG-level payment and report safety data.

**(b) Market size & volume.** As of September 2025, 419 hospitals across 147 systems and 39 states approved (figures vary 366–419 by source/date). Sustainability requires scale of ~30–60 patients/day.

**(c) Company counts & market share.** Health systems (Mayo Clinic, Mass General Brigham, Kaiser) plus enabler vendors (Medically Home, Best Buy Health/Current Health, Contessa/Amedisys).

**(d) Financial & operational benchmarks.** A 2024 CMS study found lower mortality and fewer post-discharge costs vs. brick-and-mortar. Financially fragile under traditional hospital cost structures (fixed inpatient overhead makes returns appear negative); leading systems find cost parity at scale.

**(e) Reimbursement/policy 2025–2026.** Program lapsed during the 43-day 2025 government shutdown (after Sept 30, 2025 expiration), extended to Jan 30, 2026, then the Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2026 (signed February 2026) extended the waiver five years to September 30, 2030 (based on the Hospital Inpatient Services Modernization Act, H.R. 4313, which passed the House by two-thirds voice vote in December 2025).

**(f) Key data sources.** CMS AHCAH approved-facility list; AHA fact sheets; Bipartisan Policy Center; CMS 2024 study; 42 U.S.C. § 1395cc-7.

**Chart suggestions:** Approved hospital growth → line; extension timeline → Gantt; mortality/cost comparison vs. inpatient → bar.

### 9. Post-Acute Continuum & Care Transitions

**(a) Structure & mechanics.** Four institutional post-acute settings plus home: SNF (PDPM per-diem), Inpatient Rehabilitation Facility (IRF PPS, CMG-based, "60% rule"), Long-Term Care Hospital (LTACH PPS, ≥25-day average length of stay), Home Health (PDGM 30-day), Hospice (per-diem). Referral patterns flow from acute-hospital discharge. Bundled payment models (BPCI-Advanced, CJR) create incentives to shift to lower-cost settings.

**(b) Market size & volume.** SNF ~$700–800/patient-day; IRF substantially higher per-diem; LTACH highest. The discharge mix from hospitals shifted toward home health and home over SNF post-COVID.

**(c–d) Benchmarks.** MedPAC has long recommended a unified post-acute care PPS (PAC PPS). Each setting's Medicare margin is assessed annually by MedPAC.

**(e) Policy 2025–2026.** Continued interest in site-neutral payment and a PAC PPS; bundled-payment evolution.

**(f) Key data sources.** MedPAC March reports (each PAC chapter); CMS MedPAR; cost reports.

**Chart suggestions:** PAC settings by per-diem cost → bar; hospital discharge destination mix → stacked bar/Sankey.

### 10. Personal Care / Non-Medical Home Care & Direct Care Workforce

**(a) Structure & mechanics.** Non-medical personal care (ADL/IADL assistance) funded largely by Medicaid HCBS (home- and community-based services), plus private pay and some Medicare Advantage supplemental benefits. Personal care aides have no federal training requirement (vs. a 75-hour federal minimum, plus 16 hours supervised practical training, for home health aides/CNAs).

**(b) Market size & volume.** The direct care workforce totals 5.4 million workers — home care workers ~3.2 million, residential care aides ~656,000, nursing assistants in nursing homes ~459,000. The home care workforce doubled from ~1.4 million (2014) to ~3.2 million (2024). Medicaid funds 68% of the $312.9 billion spent annually on HCBS. Home care workers assist >9.8 million people. Per PHI's "Direct Care Workers in the United States: Key Facts 2025," 9.7 million total job openings are projected 2024–2034.

**(c) Company counts & market share.** Highly fragmented; many independent providers via Medicaid consumer-direction. Agencies: Honor, Help at Home, Addus HomeCare, BrightSpring, Bayada, Senior Helpers, Home Instead.

**(d) Financial & operational benchmarks.** The median hourly wage for all direct care workers was just $17.36 in 2024 (home care $16.77); median annual earnings are just under $26,000 ($22,429 home care). 49% rely on public assistance such as Medicaid or SNAP; 36% live in low-income households. Home care turnover ~75% (2024). The workforce is predominantly women (85% of home care workers) and people of color (67% of home care workers).

**(e) Reimbursement/policy 2025–2026.** The Medicaid Access Rule "80/20" provision requires ≥80% of Medicaid payments for homemaker/home-health-aide/personal-care services to go to direct-care worker compensation. Medicaid cuts in the 2025 reconciliation law threaten rates.

**(f) Key data sources.** PHI "Direct Care Workers in the United States: Key Facts" (annual); BLS OEWS; CMS HCBS data.

**Chart suggestions:** Direct care workforce by segment → stacked bar; wage trend 2014–2024 → line; turnover → bar; job openings projection (9.7M) → bar.

### 12. Contract Research Organizations (CROs)

**(a) Structure & mechanics.** CROs provide outsourced clinical development: preclinical, Phase I–III trial management, biostatistics, regulatory. Phases: Phase I (20–100 subjects, safety/PK, several months), Phase II (100–300, efficacy, months–2 years), Phase III (300–3,000+, confirmatory, 1–4 years). ~12% of drugs entering trials reach approval (CBO).

**(b) Market size & volume.** Global CRO market ~$80–86 billion (2024) by consensus of multiple firms (full range $66B–$86B by scope: e.g., $84.3B Market Research Future, $86.3B Fortune Business Insights, $66.4B Frost & Sullivan). Outsourcing penetration ~50% of clinical development (some estimates 54% of industry-sponsored trials in 2024, up from 48% in 2020). Decentralized/virtual trials growing; ICH E6(R3) GCP adopted by FDA Sept 2025, EMA July 2025.

**(c) Major CROs (2023–2024 revenue).** IQVIA $15.4B (2024, company-reported); ICON plc ~$8.3B; Thermo Fisher/PPD (CDMO ~$7B + CRO ~$8B segments); Parexel (~$3B, private, EQT/Goldman $8.5B buyout 2021); Syneos Health (private, ~$5.4B, $7B take-private 2023); Charles River Labs $4.13B (2023, preclinical leader); Fortrea $3.1B (2023, spun from Labcorp); Medpace $2.109B (2024); WuXi AppTec. Top 10 CROs ~$34B (2022), ~69–70% of contract clinical-services revenue.

**(d) Benchmarks.** Average drug development cost ~$2.6 billion (Tufts CSDD, 2014 study, 2013 dollars; $2.87B with post-approval R&D). Per-phase trial costs ~Phase 1 $4M, Phase 2 $13M, Phase 3 $20M.

**(e–f) Policy/sources.** ICH E6(R3); IQVIA/Tufts CSDD; company filings (IQVIA, ICON, Medpace, Charles River, Fortrea).

**Chart suggestions:** CRO market size estimates by firm → bar; top CRO revenues → bar; outsourcing penetration trend → line; trial phase patient counts → bar; per-phase costs → bar.

### 13. Contract Development & Manufacturing Organizations (CDMOs)

**(a) Structure & mechanics.** Outsourced drug development and manufacturing; split between small-molecule (chemical synthesis) and biologics (mammalian/microbial cell culture, single-use bioreactors). Services span formulation, process development, clinical/commercial manufacturing, fill-finish.

**(b) Market size & volume.** Global pharma CDMO market ~$155.5 billion (2024), projected $293.6 billion by 2033 (~7.4% CAGR). Biologics CDMO ~$19–22 billion (2024). North America ~43.5% share. Biologics ~77% of large-molecule CDMO; monoclonal antibodies ~57% of biologics CDMO.

**(c) Major players.** Lonza (largest CDMO by commercialized drug approvals; acquired Roche Vacaville site for $1.2B Oct 2024 — ~330,000-L bioreactor capacity; "One Lonza" pure-play restructuring Dec 2024), Catalent (acquired by Novo Holdings $16.5B, Dec 2024), Samsung Biologics (contracts with 17 of top 20 pharma; >$3.3B new contract value in 2024), WuXi Biologics/AppTec, Thermo Fisher/Patheon, Fujifilm Diosynth, Boehringer Ingelheim, AGC Biologics.

**(d) Benchmarks.** Biologics CDMO CAGR ~9–11% (Lonza estimate) to 15%+ (some firms). Demand outpacing capacity, especially large-scale mammalian and microbial; ADC drug sales ~20% CAGR. Lonza saw a >40% increase in early-stage inquiries in 2024.

**(e–f) Policy/sources.** FDA Emerging Technology Program; company annual reports; Grand View Research; Bourne Partners; Lonza annual report.

**Chart suggestions:** CDMO market size & projection → line; biologics vs. small-molecule split → pie; major player capacity/revenue → bar; biologics CDMO CAGR → bar.

## GROUP E — OTHER SPECIALIZED VERTICALS

### 14. Medical Transportation & Logistics (NEMT)

**(a) Structure & mechanics.** Non-emergency medical transportation (NEMT) is a federally mandated Medicaid benefit since 1965. States administer via brokers or managed-care organizations; brokers contract with independent transportation providers (asset-light). Medicare Advantage increasingly offers NEMT as supplemental. Original Medicare does not cover routine NEMT (only medically necessary ambulance).

**(b) Market size & volume.** US NEMT market ~$12.77 billion (2025 estimate, Mordor) to ~$16.71 billion (2024, Insight Partners), with estimates ranging $9.8–17 billion. Medicaid NEMT spending exceeds $3 billion annually (<1% of Medicaid). Medicaid funds >60% of trips (51.72% of market by payer). ModivCare serves over 7 million Medicaid enrollees.

**(c) Company counts & market share.** ModivCare ~25–35% broker share (manages 5,000+ providers); MTM (acquired Access2Care Oct 2024 — now all 50 states, +25% revenue, 8.6 million annual rides) 15–25%; plus Verida, Southeastrans, Lyft/Uber Health. Major transit operators: Transdev, AMR.

**(d) Financial & operational benchmarks.** Per-trip economics (2023–24 Medicaid broker averages): ambulatory/sedan $18–35 base + $1.50–2.50/mile; wheelchair van $35–65 base (NY up to $72, MS as low as $28) + $2.00–3.50/mile; stretcher $75–150 base. Medicaid-only operators 10–15% net margins; diversified payer mix 20–30%. Average vehicle utilization ~35% (60–80% with AI dispatch). Driver turnover >64% annually.

**(e) Reimbursement/policy 2025–2026.** CMS 2026 rule embeds transportation experience into Medicaid quality reporting; Medicare Advantage NEMT inclusion ~36% (2025). States tightening prior-auth windows (10 business days → 7 calendar days); Oregon/NY mandatory driver certification.

**(f) Key data sources.** CMS Medicaid NEMT data; Mordor/Insight Partners market reports; ModivCare/MTM filings.

**Chart suggestions:** NEMT trip volume/market size → bar; broker market share → pie; per-trip rates by vehicle type → bar; payer mix → stacked bar.

### 15. Durable Medical Equipment (DME) / Home Medical Equipment

**(a) Structure & mechanics.** Billed via HCPCS Level II codes; covered under Medicare Part B (the DMEPOS benefit). The DMEPOS Competitive Bidding Program (CBP) sets prices in designated areas (with periodic gaps/temporary lapses). Categories: respiratory (oxygen, CPAP/BiPAP, ventilators), mobility (wheelchairs, walkers), diabetic supplies (CGMs, test strips), hospital beds, prosthetics/orthotics.

**(b) Market size & volume.** Large fragmented market; CMS DMEPOS fee schedule governs reimbursement. CGMs and diabetic supplies are the fastest-growing category.

**(c) Company counts & market share.** Major suppliers: Lincare (respiratory), Apria/Owens & Minor, AdaptHealth, Rotech; device manufacturers feeding suppliers include ResMed and Philips. Fragmented with many regional players.

**(d–e) Benchmarks/policy 2025–2026.** CMS updates the DMEPOS fee schedule and accreditation requirements annually (the CY2026 HH PPS final rule also covered DMEPOS Competitive Bidding and accreditation updates). New provider-enrollment anti-fraud provisions.

**(f) Key data sources.** CMS DMEPOS fee schedule and competitive bidding files; HCPCS code set; CMS supplier data.

**Chart suggestions:** DME categories by spend → bar; competitive bidding price impact → before/after bar; supplier market share → pie.

### 16. Dialysis & the ESRD Ecosystem

**(a) Structure & mechanics.** ESRD PPS pays a single bundled per-treatment rate (since 2011) covering all renal dialysis services, drugs, labs, supplies. The base rate is adjusted by patient-level case-mix (age, body surface area, low BMI, onset, four comorbidity categories; pediatric adjusters) and facility-level factors (wage index, low-volume, rural). Add-ons: home/self-dialysis training, outlier payments, TDAPA (drugs), TPNIES (equipment), transitional pediatric add-on (TPEAPA, 30%). Labor share 55.2%, non-labor 44.8%.

**(b) Market size & volume.** ~808,000 people living with ESKD (68–69% on dialysis, 31–32% transplant); ~550,000–560,000 dialysis patients. Medicare expects to pay ~$6 billion to ~7,600 ESRD facilities in CY2026. ~27,332 kidney transplants (2023). Home dialysis ~14.5% of prevalent patients (PD ~13%+, home HD ~0.4% of incident).

**(c) Company counts & market share.** DaVita (~2,657 US centers, ~37% share) and Fresenius (~2,600–2,800 US centers, ~38% share) control ~77% of facilities. Per Koukounas et al. (JAMA-published study, PMC12177639), DaVita and Fresenius's combined facility share rose from 59.1% (2005) to 77.1% (2019), "with 32.5% of the national population living in a hospital service area (HSA) without access to a dialysis facility other than these." Others: US Renal Care, Dialysis Clinic Inc, Satellite Healthcare. FTC opened an antitrust investigation (2024) into non-compete clauses. Baxter divested Kidney Care (Vantive) to Carlyle for $3.8B (Aug 2024).

**(d) Financial & operational benchmarks.** Adjusted mortality 187.7 per 1,000 person-years on dialysis vs. 74.3 for transplant. CY2026 adult outlier FDL $14.80, MAP $23.68. 7,582 total facilities in the proposed-rule analysis (7,523 with NAPA multiplier 1.0).

**(e) Reimbursement/policy 2025–2026.** CY2026 ESRD PPS final rule: base rate $281.71 (up from $273.82), +2.2% / ~$180 million. AKI rate $281.71. New Non-Contiguous Areas Payment Adjustment (Alaska/Hawaii/Pacific, 25% cap). The ESRD Treatment Choices (ETC) Model was terminated effective Dec 31, 2025; a JAMA 2024 study (Koukounas et al.) found facilities serving patients with two-plus social risk factors had lower home-dialysis use (14.1% vs 16.0%) and higher penalties (18.5% vs 11.5%). TDAPA continues for DefenCath, Vafseo, and phosphate binders ($36.41/monthly claim). Kidney Care Choices (KCC/CKCC) value-based models continue.

**(f) Key data sources.** USRDS Annual Data Report; CMS ESRD PPS final rules; CMS dialysis facility cost reports; Dialysis Facility Compare; NIDDK.

**Chart suggestions:** ESRD PPS bundle build-up → waterfall; dialysis patient growth → line; DaVita/Fresenius market share over time (59.1%→77.1%) → line; home vs. in-center modality → stacked bar; base rate trend → line.

### 17. Fertility / Reproductive Health & ART

**(a) Structure & mechanics.** IVF and assisted reproductive technology (ART). Largely cash-pay or employer-benefit funded; growing state mandates. A "cycle" = ovarian stimulation → egg retrieval → fertilization → embryo transfer. Regulated under the Fertility Clinic Success Rate and Certification Act of 1992 (mandatory CDC reporting via NASS).

**(b) Market size & volume.** 435,426 ART cycles on 251,542 unique patients at 457 reporting clinics (2022, CDC); 94,039 live-birth deliveries, 98,289 infants (~2.6% of all US births). 184,423 were egg/embryo banking cycles. $8.9 billion spent at fertility clinics (2023), projected ~13.6% CAGR to $16.8 billion by 2028.

**(c) Company counts & market share.** ~450+ clinics. Consolidating networks: US Fertility, Inception Fertility (Prelude), Pinnacle Fertility, Kindbody, CCRM Fertility. PE/employer-benefit-driven (Progyny, Carrot, Maven as benefit managers).

**(d) Financial & operational benchmarks.** Single IVF cycle $15,000–$20,000 (can exceed $30,000); average ~2.5 cycles to pregnancy → can exceed $40,000. OOP for successful IVF >$61,000; donor-egg IVF $38,015/cycle, $72,642/successful outcome. NASS covers ~98% of US cycles.

**(e) Reimbursement/policy 2025–2026.** ~32% of organizations offered IVF coverage to employees (2024, up from 13% in 2016 and 30% in 2020). ASRM broadened the infertility definition (2023) to include LGBTQ+ and single would-be parents. Some state mandates expanding.

**(f) Key data sources.** CDC ART/NASS; SART Clinic Summary Report; ASRM; employer-benefit surveys (IFEBP).

**Chart suggestions:** IVF cycle volume → bar; cycle cost components → stacked bar; clinic spending growth ($8.9B→$16.8B) → line; employer IVF coverage trend (13%→32%) → line; success rate by age → line.

## Recommendations

**Staged analytical priorities for building chartable datasets:**

1. **Immediate (anchor on federal payment files):** Pull the FY/CY2026 CMS final-rule rate tables for SNF, hospice, home health, and ESRD — these give exact per-diem/per-treatment base rates and the build-up factors that translate directly into waterfall charts. Benchmark to revisit: if any 2027 proposed rule diverges >2 points from market-basket inflation, re-model margins.
2. **Near-term (occupancy/volume time series):** Build quarterly time series from NIC MAP (senior living/skilled nursing occupancy), USRDS (dialysis patients/modality), and CDC NASS (ART cycles). These are the cleanest line-chart series. Threshold: senior living occupancy crossing 90% (projected end-2026) is the trigger for re-rating supply-gap narratives.
3. **Medium-term (workforce and market structure):** Use PHI (direct care), HRSA NCHWA (behavioral health), and BACB (ABA) for workforce-shortage bars; use MedPAC margin data and market-share series (dialysis, hospice, NEMT) for concentration analysis.
4. **Watch items that change the thesis:** (a) Medicaid cuts in the 2025 reconciliation law — the single biggest downside risk for SNF, personal care, NEMT, and ABA (all Medicaid-dependent); (b) any revival of SNF staffing rules; (c) home health PDGM clawback of the deferred ~$5.3B (will pressure CY2027+); (d) gene-therapy commercial viability after the Roctavian/Beqvez withdrawals.
5. **For visualization consistency:** Standardize per-unit economics into comparable units (per patient-day, per treatment, per cycle, per trip, per hour) for a cross-vertical "unit economics" comparison chart — the single most useful synthesis visual. Anchor values: SNF ~$700–800/patient-day; hospice RHC ~$224/day; ESRD $281.71/treatment; IVF $15,000–$20,000/cycle; NEMT $18–65/trip; gene therapy $373,000–$4.25M/dose.

## Caveats

- **Market-size figures vary widely by source and scope**, especially for NEMT ($9.8–17B), CRO ($66–86B), and CDMO (biologics $19–22B). These are commercial market-research estimates with differing definitions; treat ranges as ranges, not point estimates. Federal/company-reported figures (CMS payments, IQVIA $15.4B, Medpace $2.109B, ESRD $6B) are more reliable.
- **Gene therapy list prices (WAC) differ materially from net/paid prices** due to outcomes-based and installment agreements (e.g., Zolgensma net ~$1.36M vs. $2.1M list). Some payer formulary sheets show updated CAR-T prices ($399K–$424K) above launch list for Yescarta/Tecartus.
- **Some figures are dated:** the $2.6B drug-development cost is the 2014 Tufts estimate (2013 dollars); SNF nursing-hours and certain hospice LOS figures reflect 2018–2023 data; SNF chain-concentration (25 chains = 19%) draws on an older IQVIA figure.
- **Policy is in flux:** the SNF staffing-mandate rescission, ETC termination, and hospital-at-home extension are confirmed, but home health clawback timing, future PDGM/PDPM parity adjustments, and Medicaid reconciliation impacts remain to be fully implemented.
- **Hospital-at-home approved-facility counts vary (366–419)** by source and date due to the lapse/re-approval cycle.
- **ABA and behavioral-health per-unit reimbursement rates are payer- and state-specific** and were not uniformly available; CPT structure is national but dollar conversions vary.
- **DME category-level spend and precise SNF per-patient-day by payer** could not be pinned to a single authoritative figure within this research cycle; the metric and its data source (CMS DMEPOS fee schedule; SNF cost reports) are flagged rather than estimated.
- Where a specific per-unit dollar figure could not be verified, the metric and its data source are flagged rather than fabricated.
